# The Church of Ink — Brand Foundation

This document captures the full context behind The Church of Ink — who Madi is,
what the brand represents, how every creative and theological decision was made,
and the thinking that led to each element of the brand language. This is the
document you read to understand *why*. AGENTS.md tells you *what*.

---

## Who Madi Is

Madi Jerrow is 17 years old. She lives in Spokane Valley, Washington, and was
raised in a strong Christian home by her parents MJ and Becky Jerrow, alongside
her sister Kenzie. Her faith is genuinely her own — not inherited passively, but
examined, tested, and held with conviction. She attends church, writes about her
faith with theological depth that would be notable in someone twice her age, and
anchors her worldview in scripture she has clearly spent time with, not just
memorized.

### The artist

She draws on skin — her own and other people's — with pens, creating detailed,
realistic artwork that people routinely mistake for real tattoos. Her style is
dark, organic, detailed: fine-line ink work with stipple shading, splatter
texture, and flowing forms. Serpents, squid, florals, plague doctors, stars,
botanical elements. Full back pieces, full leg pieces, arm sleeves — all drawn
freehand with pens, on skin, because as she puts it: "Skin just feels different
when you draw on it."

She drew a plague doctor portrait ("Black Death") at 14 — signed and dated —
with pencil work that showed genuine draftsmanship: proportion, perspective on
the hat brim, leather texture, anatomical accuracy. That's not doodling, that's
a 14-year-old who takes her work seriously. By 17, the progression from that
pencil sketch to the full-body ink work she produces now is significant. She is
going to be genuinely dangerous with a tattoo machine.

She already draws on friends — one photo was captioned "when ur bestie runs out
of space on her own legs so u get free trial tats." She's building a client base
informally, practicing her craft on willing canvases before she ever picks up a
tattoo machine. Her art Instagram handle is @madi_art69.

### The person

She dresses in all black. Her hair is purple — that's her signature. Striped
socks, lace tights, corset belts, platform shoes. She carries a fuzzy pink
teddy bear bag to a theme park. She flexes in front of her blue Jeep Rubicon
covered in mud after off-roading. She eats ice cream on a sidewalk with braces
and a peace sign. She sits in an immersive light exhibit with projected flowers
washing over her skin. She is playful and bold and does not take herself too
seriously, even though her work is serious.

The brand cannot be only dark and moody — there is a rugged, outdoor, Pacific
Northwest dimension to who she is. She drives a Jeep Rubicon through mud and
snow, spends 50+ days a season on a mountain. The aesthetic is dark, but the
person behind it is physical, adventurous, and alive.

### The professional

She works as a ski instructor and has already earned her PSIA Level 1
certification — a professional credential that is not a participation badge.
It involves prep work, coursework, and study leading up to a two-day on-snow
assessment where both skiing ability and teaching ability are evaluated against
a defined standard. She is 17.

When she graduates high school in 2027 — at which point she will simultaneously
hold her associate's degree in business — she plans to go to New Zealand for the
southern hemisphere "summer" to work as a ski instructor there. Tattoo artistry
is her long-term calling, but she is building a broader professional life in
parallel. The ski instructing demonstrates the same qualities the brand is built
on: discipline, certification, teaching ability, and the willingness to do hard
things before the world is ready to take her seriously.

She is not one-dimensional, and the brand cannot be either.

### The brand as ministry

MJ stated directly during the brand development process: "She sees the brand
as a way to bring ministry work into a space that doesn't see Christian
influence much." This is the mission beneath the brand. The tattoo chair as
mission field. Not preaching — presence. Loving people in a space where they
don't expect it from a Christian.

## What She Believes

Madi has written two substantial pieces that reveal her theological framework
and her vision for tattoo artistry as a vocation. These writings are the
primary source material for the brand language.

### On the church and judgment

Her first piece addresses why young adults are leaving the church. Her thesis
is direct: they are not leaving because they stopped caring about faith. They
are leaving because they feel judged by the people who are supposed to represent
Christ. She writes from personal experience — she has felt the weight of
people judging her based on her appearance before taking the time to know her.
She names the tension plainly: she is part of the generation that churches want
to reach but are pushing away.

She grounds this in scripture: James 2 on favoritism, Matthew 7:1 on judgment,
John 13:35 on love as the defining mark of a disciple. She references the
Casting Crowns song "If We Are the Body" — written for a youth group, rooted in
James 2 — and uses its core question to illustrate the exact failure she's
describing: "If we are the body, why aren't His arms reaching? Why aren't His
hands healing? Why is His love not showing them there is a way?" A traveler
walks into a church looking for something, and the weight of judgmental looks
sends him back out. He came in from the world hoping the church would be
different, and the church sent him back to it.

Her conclusion is not bitter or angry. It is clear-eyed and constructive:
"Judgment belongs to God; our responsibility is love." If the church wants the
next generation to stay, it must stop pushing people away with criticism and
start showing — not just telling — what Christ's love actually looks like.

### On tattoo artistry as vocation

Her second piece makes the case that tattoo artistry is a legitimate,
recognized profession — citing the Bureau of Labor Statistics (Craft and Fine
Artists category), the CDC (health and safety standards), and IBISWorld
(industry revenue and growth data). She addresses the Leviticus 19:28 concern
head-on, contextualizing it within Old Testament ceremonial law given to
Israelites in a specific historical context of pagan body-marking practices.
She notes that Jesus and the apostles never reaffirm the tattoo prohibition,
and cites Mark 7:15, Colossians 2:16, and Romans 6:14 to argue that holiness
under the new covenant comes from Christ, not from the body's markings.

She then pivots to the theological heart of the brand. Drawing on Jordan
Raynor's "The Sacredness of Secular Work," she argues that God does not divide
life into sacred and secular categories. Work done with excellence, integrity,
and a heart to serve — in any profession — matters deeply to God. Tattoo
artistry, practiced responsibly and with the right intention, is not outside
God's purposes. It is a professional craft and an artistic form of service. It
creates daily opportunities to treat people with dignity, to listen, to
encourage, and to reflect Christ-like character in a space where many may never
encounter the church.

Her closing line: "When done with the right heart and purpose, it becomes
meaningful service — work offered to God and used by Him to serve people."

This is not a teenager rationalizing a rebellious career choice. This is a
young woman building a theological framework for her calling.

## Why "The Church of Ink"

The name itself is a statement. It claims sacred space in a place the
traditional church doesn't usually go. It says: the tattoo chair can be a
place of ministry. The ink studio can be a space where people feel loved
instead of judged. The work itself — art on skin — can be an act of worship.

The name will make some Christians uncomfortable. That's part of the point.
The discomfort is exactly the judgment problem Madi writes about. If the name
makes you flinch, you're proving her thesis.

The brand is not a ministry organization. It is not a church. It is a tattoo
artistry brand that is unapologetically rooted in Christian faith — not as
marketing, but as the foundation of why and how the work is done. Madi wants to
bring ministry-minded presence into a space that doesn't typically see Christian
influence. Not by preaching. By loving people in a space where they don't
expect it.

## How the Brand Language Was Built

Every element of the brand language was derived from Madi's own words, beliefs,
and scripture. Nothing was invented from the outside. The process was
iterative — multiple drafts, tested against her voice, refined until each line
felt like something she would actually say.

### Tagline: "Sacred work, inscribed on skin."

**How we got here:** The tagline went through several iterations. Early versions
used "written on skin" but MJ identified the hangup — "written" implies text
and words, not art. What Madi does isn't writing; it's drawing, marking,
creating.

We explored alternatives systematically:
- "drawn" — most literal and honest, but felt flat
- "carried" — the person takes the art with them; echoes carrying your cross
- "marked" — direct tattoo language, but "marked" carries negative connotation
  (branded, judged — ironically the problem she's fighting against)
- "etched" — permanence is clear, but cold and mechanical, not her energy
- "permanent" — strong but clinical
- "lived" — poetic but reaching
- "worn" — shifts focus to the receiver, not the act

We then explored biblical language around permanence. The breakthrough came from
searching for scriptural words that carried the weight of something eternal,
permanent, and living:

"Inscribed" comes from Isaiah 49:16: "See, I have inscribed you on the palms
of my hands." This verse is almost too perfect for a tattoo brand rooted in
faith. God marks his people on his own skin. The inscription on God's palm is
the ultimate permanent mark — and it was done out of love. The word carries
theological weight, artistic precision, and permanence in a single term.

Other biblical options explored before landing on "inscribed":
- "engraved" — same Isaiah 49:16 in some translations, but heavier than needed
- "sealed" — Song of Solomon 8:6, "set me as a seal upon your arm," but too
  formal
- "living" — 2 Corinthians 3:3, "written not with ink but with the Spirit of
  the living God, not on tablets of stone but on tablets of human hearts" — 
  beautiful but too abstract
- "enduring" — 1 Peter 1:25, "the word of the Lord endures forever" — generic
- "abiding" — the Greek word menō from 1 Corinthians 13:13 ("these three
  remain") is the same word translated "abide" in John 15 — theologically
  rich but doesn't read naturally in the tagline

MJ's response when "inscribed" landed: "That's the one." And the theological
defense writes itself: if anyone challenges the faith-and-tattoo tension,
Isaiah 49:16 is the answer. God inscribes people on his own palms.

The tagline says three things at once: the work is sacred (not secular), it
is inscribed (permanent, intentional, with the weight of Isaiah 49:16), and
it lives on skin (the canvas is alive, the art moves with the person).

### Creed: "Art done with heart and integrity is an offering — to the person wearing it and to God who gave the talent."

**How we got here:** The creed went through the most iterations of any brand
element. The challenge was combining the professional standard, the theological
claim, and the brand declaration into something that flowed naturally.

**First attempt — three separate ideas:**
> Skin feels different when you draw on it. Art done with heart and integrity
> is an offering — to the person wearing it and to God who gave the talent.
> The Church of Ink is where craft becomes calling.

MJ's feedback: "It just doesn't flow... it is three different ideas mashed
together in a paragraph." He was right.

**Second attempt — compressed:**
> Art done with heart and integrity is an offering — to the person wearing it
> and to God who gave the talent. Where vocation becomes worship — that's where
> The Church of Ink lives. Sacred work, written on skin.

Still felt robotic and jerky.

**Third attempt — one breath:**
> Work done with excellence, ethical care, and a heart to serve is work offered
> to God. The Church of Ink exists where art becomes vocation, and vocation
> becomes worship — sacred work, written on skin.

MJ flagged: "the creed doesn't flow — is very robotic and jerky." The list
structure ("excellence, ethical care, and a heart to serve") read like a
mission statement checklist.

**The breakthrough** came from recognizing the three ideas were doing different
jobs and didn't all belong in the creed:

- "Skin feels different when you draw on it" is the origin story — personal,
  visceral, why she does this. It stands alone as the origin quote.
- "God does not divide life into sacred and secular" is the doctrine — but the
  creed already communicates this idea without stating it explicitly. "Vocation
  becomes worship" IS the no-sacred-secular-divide, just landed better. The
  explicit doctrinal statement is the theology lesson; the creed is the
  conviction. She doesn't need to explain the doctrine — she just lives it.
- The creed needed to be one thought about what she believes about the work.

MJ identified the strongest line from all the drafts: "Art done with heart and
integrity is an offering — to the person wearing it and to God who gave the
talent." That single sentence carries everything. "Art done with heart and
integrity" is the professional standard. "Is an offering" makes the theological
claim. "To the person wearing it" grounds it in service. "And to God who gave
the talent" completes the loop.

**The "the God" correction:** An earlier version said "to the God who gave the
talent." MJ caught it immediately: "the God infers different gods —
Christianity is about God, singular, no other." The article was removed. That
kind of theological precision matters in a faith-rooted brand.

### Ethos: "Do the work. Let your character speak louder than perception."

**How we got here:** This line emerged from MJ's LinkedIn post about Madi
passing her PSIA Level 1 certification (full text preserved in the "A father's
perspective" section above). The post contained this observation:

"Sometimes credibility shows up much earlier. Sometimes it looks like
preparation, composure, humility, and the willingness to do hard things before
the world is quite ready to take you seriously."

And this about Madi specifically:

"She knows what it feels like to be judged too quickly, whether because of age,
appearance, or assumptions. But she also knows how to stay grounded in who she
is, do the work, and let character and capability speak louder than perception."

The ethos distills that into a directive: do the work (discipline, preparation,
professional standard), then let your character speak louder than perception
(don't argue with the judges — outwork them).

**Iteration:** The first version was "Do the work. Let character speak louder
than perception." MJ added "your" — "Do the work, then let your character speak
louder than perception — or something like that." The final version kept "your"
but dropped "then" to make it punchier: "Do the work. Let your character speak
louder than perception."

This is how Madi actually operates. She doesn't argue about whether her style
or age or appearance should be accepted. She shows up, does the work, earns the
credential, produces the art, and lets the results speak.

### Origin quote: "Skin feels different when you draw on it."

This is Madi's own words — the most authentic, visceral thing she has said
about why she does what she does. It's not theological. It's not professional.
It's the raw, physical reason she is drawn to this medium. Skin is alive. It
responds differently than paper or canvas. It moves, it stretches, it has
texture and warmth. For Madi, that difference is the beginning of everything.

This quote doesn't appear on the landing page. It lives in the brand
documentation as the origin point — the "why" beneath the "why."

### Scripture anchors

Four verses form the theological backbone:

**Isaiah 49:16** — "See, I have inscribed you on the palms of my hands."
The tagline foundation. God marks his people on skin. The nail wounds of the
crucifixion are the ultimate inscription — permanent marks made out of love.
This verse makes the entire brand theologically bulletproof. If anyone
challenges the faith-and-tattoo tension, this is the answer.

**1 Timothy 4:12** — "Don't let anyone look down on you because you are young,
but set an example for the believers in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith
and in purity." Both Madi and MJ independently anchor to this verse when
describing who she is. She cites it in her church essay. MJ references it in
his LinkedIn post about her PSIA certification. It is the identity verse —
youth is not a disqualifier for credibility. The independent convergence on
this verse is notable — it wasn't coordinated, it's shared conviction.

**Colossians 3:23** — "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as
working for the Lord, not for human masters." The vocation verse. Madi cites
this directly in her tattoo artistry essay as the capstone of her argument
that all honest work, done with excellence, is sacred. This verse bridges the
gap between secular profession and sacred purpose.

**1 Corinthians 13:13** — "And now these three remain: faith, hope and love.
But the greatest of these is love." The mission verse. MJ brought this verse
into the conversation — asking "where does 1 Corinthians 13:13 come into any
of this?" It fits as the deepest anchor: the one that explains *why* she wants
to be in the tattoo chair. Not to preach. To love people in a space where they
don't expect it from a Christian. Her entire church essay builds to the argument
that love, not judgment, is the church's responsibility. The other verses
explain *what* (youth identity) and *how* (work as worship). This one explains
*why* (love above all).

Together: youth + vocation + love = Madi's brand.

### Revelation — explored but not anchored

During the brand development process, we explored whether Revelation contained
relevant imagery. It does:

- **Revelation 19:16** — "On his robe and on his thigh he has this name
  written: King of Kings and Lord of Lords." Jesus has a name written on his
  skin. That is, by definition, a tattoo verse.
- **Revelation 2:17** — "I will give each of them a white stone with a new
  name written on it, known only to the one who receives it." A permanent mark
  of identity, personal, given by God.
- **Revelation 22:4** — "They will see his face, and his name will be on their
  foreheads." His name marked on skin, again.

The Bible's final book describes God marking his people on skin repeatedly.
This is theologically significant for a tattoo brand. However, these verses
were not included in the primary scripture anchors because:

1. Revelation imagery tends toward the apocalyptic, which pulls the brand into
   a different aesthetic than where Madi lives
2. The four primary anchors already cover identity, vocation, mission, and the
   tagline — the Revelation verses would be additive, not foundational
3. They are kept here as theological ammunition — useful for future content,
   about pages, or responding to critics, but not part of the core brand stack

## Visual Identity Decisions

### Why dark

Madi wears all black. Her room is lit with blue LED strips. Her mirror selfies
are midnight-blue darkness with ink art glowing on skin. The dark aesthetic is
not an affectation — it's her actual environment. The brand should feel like
stepping into her space.

### Why purple

Her hair is purple. It's her signature — the one pop of color against the all-
black wardrobe. Purple is the accent color because it's authentically hers.
Used sparingly on the page — atmospheric, not decorative. It should feel like
it's bleeding through the darkness, not painted on top.

### Why Cormorant Garamond

The brand needs a serif with sacred, editorial weight — something that feels
intentional and elevated without being corporate or churchy. Cormorant Garamond
has a light weight (300) that gives the airy, inscriptive quality the wordmark
needs. Its italic is beautiful for the tagline. It's free on Google Fonts and
not overused in the tattoo/alternative space.

### Why the page "loads" and doesn't "appear"

MJ's direction was specific and intentional: "I want the page to have identity,
motion, load style — I want the page to 'load', not appear." He was deliberately
vague on the specifics because the feeling mattered more than the technique.
The solution: a reveal sequence where elements arrive with intention and timing,
like someone drawing on skin. The wordmark inscribes itself (clip-path wipe,
not fade). The divider draws from center outward. The tagline drifts up from
darkness. This is a page about an artist who draws things into existence — the
page itself should feel drawn into existence.

### Logo direction (in progress)

Multiple logo directions were explored, refined, and in some cases killed.
The full exploration trail is documented here for context.

**Direction A — "The Bloom":** A single flower drawn in dark, detailed,
stipple-texture style — her recurring floral motif. Rejected because the
AI-generated versions came out too pretty, too botanical-illustration, too
garden-magazine. MJ's feedback: "the bloom just doesn't capture her mildly edgy
style of who she is as it was drawn." The bloom was too polished and delicate
for someone who drew a plague doctor at 14.

**Direction B — "The Hand":** An open palm with ink flowing from fingertips.
Tied to Isaiah 49:16. This evolved through several critical iterations:

- First AI-generated hand was dramatic and compelling in style but lacked
  Christian specificity — could read as gothic or occult
- MJ identified the key pivot: "what if it was closer to nail in the palm for
  the crucifixion?" This changed everything. The nail wound in the center of
  the palm ties to both Isaiah 49:16 (inscribed on the palms) AND the
  crucifixion (the nail as the first inscription on skin)
- Second round added botanical elements growing from the nail wound — beauty
  from sacrifice, art flowing from the wound
- **Critical guardrail identified:** without the nail clearly present, the open
  palm drifts into tarot/palmistry imagery. One version with a lotus-like bloom
  replacing the nail was flagged: "does this get too close to tarot?" Yes — the
  nail is the theological anchor that keeps it Christian
- The nail must be unmistakable. No eye imagery, no celestial symbols, no
  mandalas. The nail does the theological work. The flowers do the beauty work.
  The drop does the ink/craft work.

**Direction C — "The Serpent & Sacred":** A snake coiling into a cross shape or
wrapping around the word "INK." Killed early. MJ correctly identified: "while
she enjoys drawing snakes it is mainly from the ability to have something that
she can continue drawing as it wraps around the body — the serpent could be
misleading with the church reference." Snake + "Church" sends an entirely
different theological message than intended.

**Direction D — "Pure Wordmark":** No icon, just hand-lettered "THE CHURCH OF
INK" with pen-on-skin texture. Explored via SVG and AI generation but neither
tool could capture the organic, hand-drawn quality needed. Remains viable as a
secondary lockup.

**Current status:** The nail-in-palm concept with botanical growth is the
strongest direction. However, AI-generated versions keep drifting — either too
clean/pretty or too dark/occult. Madi's specific aesthetic lives in a tension
between dark/edgy and faith-rooted that AI struggles to hold. The strongest
version of the logo is one Madi draws herself, using AI concepts as reference.

**Logo lockup options explored:**
1. Stacked — mark above, text below (recommended primary)
2. Seal/badge — text arcing around the mark (good for merch/social)
3. Inscribed on palm — text integrated into the hand's skin lines
4. Horizontal — mark left, text right (headers/banners)

The hand mark alone serves as icon/favicon. Full lockup with text for larger
formats.

## The "Sacred/Secular" Line — Why It Didn't Make the Cut

"God does not divide life into sacred and secular" was a strong theological idea
pulled from Madi's writing (via Raynor's framework). It was explored as a
potential creed element. MJ acknowledged it was a good idea but said it "doesn't
fit anywhere" in the brand language stack.

The reason: the creed already communicates this principle without stating it
explicitly. "Art done with heart and integrity is an offering — to the person
wearing it and to God who gave the talent" IS the no-sacred-secular-divide,
just expressed as conviction rather than doctrine. The explicit statement is the
theology lesson; the creed is the lived belief. She doesn't need to explain the
doctrine — she just lives it.

The line is preserved here as theological context. It may be useful for future
content — blog posts, about pages, or responses to critics — but it does not
belong in the core brand language stack.

## What This Brand Is Not

- Not a ministry organization or a church
- Not apologizing for tattoos or asking permission from the Christian community
- Not preachy, not evangelistic in tone, not trying to convert anyone
- Not corporate, not polished, not vector-clean
- Not a teenager's side project — this is a serious brand for a serious artist
  who happens to be 17
- Not separate from faith — faith is the foundation, not the marketing
- Not only about tattoo art — Madi is a ski instructor, a business student, an
  off-roader, an adventurer. The brand represents one dimension of a
  multidimensional person

## What This Brand Is

A tattoo artistry brand that is unapologetically rooted in Christian faith.
The work is sacred. The canvas is skin. The artist is young, talented,
disciplined, and grounded in conviction. The brand exists where craft becomes
calling, and calling becomes worship. It is a flag planted in a space the
church doesn't usually enter — not to preach, but to love people where they
are.

Sacred work, inscribed on skin.
